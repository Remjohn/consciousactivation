# **Cognitive Dynamics of Short-Form Video: An LC4MP-Based Framework for Quantifying Media Processing and Editorial Complexity**

The evolution of digital media consumption, characterized by the meteoric rise of high-velocity, short-form video content on platforms such as TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts, has outpaced traditional methods of assessing viewer engagement and cognitive burden. To transition from a subjective, intuition-based "Cognitive Load Score" to a rigorous, research-grounded framework, it is necessary to apply the principles of the Limited Capacity Model of Motivated Mediated Message Processing (LC4MP). Developed primarily by Annie Lang, this model provides a bio-psychological lens through which to observe how the human information-processing system interacts with the structural and content features of modern media.1 By examining the metabolic and cognitive constraints of the viewer, the LC4MP offers a quantifiable approach to understanding how specific editing techniques—such as rapid cuts, split-screen compositions, and speed ramps—tax the limited resources available for encoding, storing, and retrieving information.4

## **Theoretical Architecture of the LC4MP**

The LC4MP is predicated on five fundamental assumptions that define the interaction between the medium and the mind. First, humans are conceptualized as limited-capacity information processors.2 This means that at any given moment, an individual possesses a finite pool of cognitive resources dedicated to processing their environment. Second, the model identifies two primary motivational systems—the appetitive (approach) and the aversive (avoidance)—which evolve to assist humans in pursuing rewards and avoiding threats.2 Third, mediated messages consist of continuous, variably redundant streams of sensory information.5 Fourth, communication is an ongoing, dynamic interaction over time.2 Finally, information processing involves three distinct but concurrent sub-processes: encoding, storage, and retrieval.1

### **The Three Cognitive Sub-processes**

Encoding is the initial stage where sensory information is selected from the environment to create a mental representation in working memory.2 This process is not a perfect replication of the stimulus; rather, it is idiosyncratic and filtered through the viewer's existing mental schemas.2 Encoding is primarily cued by structural features that trigger the orienting response (OR), such as sudden camera cuts or auditory changes.3

Storage involves the process of linking newly encoded mental representations to long-term memory structures.2 The LC4MP utilizes a spreading activation associative network model to explain this, where information is better stored when more links are formed between new data and previously held knowledge.2 If the encoding process fails or is shallow, storage is inherently compromised because there is no robust representation to link to long-term stores.2

Retrieval is the continuous reactivation of stored information, which is essential for making sense of incoming content.1 Retrieval is both a tool used during processing and a measure of the final memory trace. When cognitive resources are stretched too thin during the encoding or storage phases, the system's ability to retrieve information later is diminished.2

### **Resource Allocation and the Subtracting Logic**

The LC4MP distinguishes between automatic and controlled resource allocation.2 Controlled allocation is driven by the viewer's goals, while automatic allocation is triggered by the message's structural and emotional properties.3 A critical concept in this model is the relationship between resources required, resources allocated, and resources available.11 This is often expressed through the logic:

## **Psychophysiological Measurement of Cognitive Load**

The transition to a research-grounded framework requires the use of validated physiological and behavioral indices to quantify the resource-load of various video features.

### **Secondary Task Reaction Time (STRT)**

The STRT is the gold-standard behavioral measure for assessing available cognitive resources.11 In a typical experiment, participants watch a primary video while responding as quickly as possible to a secondary probe, such as a tone or a visual flash.11 Slower reaction times indicate that the primary task is consuming more resources, leaving fewer available for the secondary task.11

Interestingly, research has identified a non-linear relationship during extreme complexity. While reaction times generally lengthen as complexity increases, they can paradoxically become faster during "cognitive overload".11 This inversion happens because the viewer effectively ceases processing the complex primary stimulus, and the few remaining resources are directed entirely toward the simple secondary task.11

### **Heart Rate and the Orienting Response**

Heart rate (HR) provides a real-time window into the orienting response.3 A sudden change in the video (a cut or a new graphic) typically triggers a brief deceleration in heart rate, which signifies the automatic allocation of resources to the encoding sub-process.3 Conversely, accelerated heart rates are associated with behavioral activation and emotional arousal.13

### **Skin Conductance and Emotional Arousal**

Skin conductance, or electrodermal activity, is the primary measure of sympathetic nervous system activation, reflecting the arousal level of the content.9 High arousal levels generally increase the total resources allocated to the message, but they also increase the resources required, potentially hastening the onset of overload if not managed correctly.9

## **Mapping Production Techniques to Cognitive Sub-processes**

Specific video editing effects and scene compositions directly impact different channels of the limited-capacity system. Establishing a "Cognitive Load Score" (CLS) requires mapping these techniques to encoding, storage, and retrieval.

### **Fast Cuts and Pacing: The Encoding Burden**

Pacing, defined as the frequency of camera changes per second, is the most powerful structural feature affecting encoding.9 Each cut triggers an orienting response, forcing the brain to allocate resources to identify the new visual field.3

The complexity of a cut is further quantified by the "Information Introduced" (II) per cut.5 A cut is not merely a temporal marker; it is a delivery mechanism for new data. The LC4MP identifies seven dimensions of visual information that can accompany a cut:

1. Object change  
2. New object  
3. Distance change  
4. Perspective change  
5. Form change  
6. Content change  
7. Spatial change 5

A cut that changes multiple dimensions simultaneously (e.g., a "perspective change" plus a "new object") requires significantly more resources to encode than a simple "distance change" within the same scene.10

### **On-Screen Text and Graphic Overlays: Dual-Channel Storage**

The inclusion of on-screen text in short-form video creates a multimedia processing scenario. According to the redundancy effect, if the text is identical to the audio narration, it may reduce the cognitive load by providing a "cueing" effect that guides attention.19 However, if the text provides new, non-redundant information, it forces the viewer to split attention between the auditory and visual channels, placing a heavy burden on the storage process as the brain attempts to integrate these two disparate streams.20

### **Split-Screen and "Sludge Content"**

The "sludge content" phenomenon—where two or more unrelated videos play simultaneously—is a hallmark of modern TikTok feeds.22 Theoretically, this should represent the pinnacle of cognitive overload.7 However, recent studies have found that memory for the primary video is often unaffected by the presence of a muted secondary "distractor" video.22 This suggests that viewers may possess a higher-than-expected capacity for "perceptual filtering" when the secondary load is muted and irrelevant.22 Yet, when the secondary video contains high-arousal content or complex motion, it inevitably competes for the same limited pool of encoding resources, eventually degrading the comprehension of the primary message.7

### **Speed Ramps and Motion**

Speed ramps (accelerating or decelerating playback) disrupt the natural rhythm of motion, which the brain is hardwired to track for survival.17 Accelerated playback (e.g., 1.5x) increases the cognitive load per second because more information is presented in a shorter time frame.24 While high-ability students may benefit from slightly faster speeds by matching their internal processing speed, most viewers experience a significant increase in cognitive load and a potential decrease in learning outcomes as playback speed moves beyond 1.25x.24

| Technique | Process Loaded | Mechanism | CLS Impact (1-5) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Rapid Cuts (\<1s)** | Encoding | Constant Orienting Responses | High (4-5) |
| **Non-Redundant Text** | Storage | Split-Attention Effect | Moderate (3) |
| **Bullet Screens** | Encoding | Visual Distraction/Search | High (4) |
| **Single Actor Casting** | Encoding/Storage | Identity Tracking Reduction | Low (1-2) |
| **Speed Ramps (\>1.25x)** | Encoding/Storage | Information Density Increase | Moderate-High (3-4) |
| **Slow Motion** | Storage | Signal of Importance/Cueing | Low-Moderate (2) |

## **Short-Form Mobile-First Content and Advertising Dynamics**

The constraints of the "swipeable" mobile feed necessitate a unique application of the LC4MP, particularly in advertising and educational content under three minutes.

### **The 400ms Imprint and the First Second**

In mobile environments, the window for engagement is drastically compressed. Research indicates that the human brain requires only 400 milliseconds to engage with a mobile advertisement and trigger a cognitive imprint, whether positive or negative.27 This "First Second Strategy" is crucial; if the initial cognitive load is too high (due to visual clutter or confusing hooks), the viewer will swipe away before the encoding process can even begin.27

### **The "One Face" Advantage**

A significant study applying the LC4MP to short-form ads found that using a single actor to play multiple roles (through costume changes) is more effective than using multiple actors.7 Humans use effortful configural processing to individuate new faces.7 By maintaining a single recognizable face, the ad reduces the "identity tracking" load, freeing up cognitive resources for message comprehension and storage.7 This reduction in load leads to higher "processing fluency," which facilitates judgments of brand authenticity and increases purchase intention.7

### **Prospective Memory and the TikTok Effect**

A critical challenge for short-form video is its impact on prospective memory—the ability to remember and execute an intention after an interruption.29 Between-subjects experiments have shown that TikTok significantly degrades prospective memory performance compared to other platforms like YouTube or Twitter.29 The combination of high multi-modal engagement, rapid context switching, and the short duration of the videos appears to deplete the resources necessary to "hold" an intention in the background while processing new content.29

## **Practical Thresholds for Cognitive Overload**

To replace intuition with data, it is necessary to identify the specific points where cognitive load turns into failure.

### **Pacing and Arousal Interaction**

The threshold for cognitive overload is not fixed; it moves depending on the emotional content of the message.9 A key finding in LC4MP research is that **cognitive overload occurs at a slower rate of pacing for arousing messages compared to calm messages**.9

For "calm" content, increasing production pacing (up to a point) can actually improve attention and memory by eliciting more ORs.9 However, for "arousing" content, recognition memory is highest when the pacing is slow and decreases steadily as the pacing increases.9 This suggests that if the content is high-stakes or emotionally intense, the editorial pacing must be slowed down to prevent the system from crashing.9

### **Cut Rate Thresholds**

While "absolute" cut-per-second rules are rare in published literature due to the interaction with information density, established patterns suggest:

* **Low Load:** 0.1 to 0.3 cuts per second (shots held for 3-10 seconds).  
* **Moderate Load:** 0.5 to 1.0 cuts per second (shots held for 1-2 seconds).  
* **Overload Risk:** Exceeding 1.5 cuts per second (shots held for \<0.7 seconds) especially when combined with high Information Introduced (Vii \> 4 per cut).5

### **Active Attention Thresholds in Mobile Video**

In the context of mobile-first feeds, ad length significantly impacts "active attention" 31:

* 15-second spots yield approximately **3.3 seconds** of active attention.31  
* 30-second spots yield only **1.2 seconds** of active attention.31  
* 6-second ads can deliver **60%** of the impact of a 30-second ad.32

This data implies that as video length increases, viewers shift from active, resource-heavy processing to passive, heuristic-based processing, or they simply disengage.31

## **Interactions of Structural and Content Features**

Total processing load is a product of the interaction between structural features (cuts, movement) and content features (emotional valence, novelty, relevance).5

### **Positivity Offset and Negativity Bias**

The LC4MP incorporates findings from motivational psychology regarding the "positivity offset" (the tendency to be more active in a neutral environment) and the "negativity bias" (the tendency of the aversive system to activate more quickly in response to threats).3

During positive messages, encoding generally increases while storage decreases over time.8 During negative messages, encoding decreases and storage increases.8 This implies that if the goal of a video is to ensure the viewer *stores* specific facts (e.g., a health warning), a negative or aversive frame might be more effective, provided the structural load is kept low enough to allow that storage to occur.3

### **Arousal and Time Perception**

Emotional arousal changes how viewers perceive the passage of time. High-arousal stimuli increase the speed of the internal "clock," leading viewers to overestimate the duration of a video.14 In short-form video, this can be a double-edged sword: high arousal ensures the video "feels" significant, but it also increases the perceived time-cost of watching, potentially leading to earlier abandonment of the clip.34

## **Theoretical Refinements and Modern Updates (2010s–2020s)**

The LC4MP has undergone significant updates to address the complexities of modern, multimodal communication.

### **The Unitary Pool Criticism**

The original LC4MP (2000) assumed that audio and visual information draw from a **single, central resource pool**.5 However, recent research suggests that cognitive resources are meaningfully separable by modality.5 This is known as the **interacting resource pools** update.36

This update posits that:

* Perceptual resources are modality-specific (visual vs. auditory).5  
* Cognitive resources (higher-order thinking, executive control) are more centralized.5  
* Interference occurs when two tasks compete for the *same* pool (e.g., two visual tasks like watching a video and reading bullet screen text).5

### **Metabolic and Spatial Constraints**

Modern refinements move away from the "pie" metaphor toward more material mechanisms for resource limits.5 Resources are now conceptualized in terms of **metabolic limits** (glucose and oxygen consumption in the brain), **spatial constraints** (the plateauing of neural firing in specific regions), and **temporal constraints** (the speed at which neurons can reset after firing).5

### **Integration with Cognitive Load Theory (CLT)**

There is an increasing trend of integrating the LC4MP with Cognitive Load Theory (CLT), particularly for educational and instructional video.7 This integration highlights the difference between **intrinsic load** (the inherent difficulty of the topic) and **extraneous load** (the unnecessary load created by poor editing or distracting graphics).7 For creators, the goal is to minimize extraneous load (using consistent casting and redundant cues) to free up resources for managing intrinsic load.7

## **Quantifying the "Cognitive Load Score" (CLS): A Proposed Framework**

Based on the LC4MP data, a research-grounded 1–5 scale for video effects and templates should be structured as follows.

### **CLS 1: Minimal Resource Demand**

* **Characteristics:** Static shots or slow pacing (\>5s per shot). High redundancy between audio and visual. Single recognizable face. Consistent lighting and backgrounds.  
* **Cognitive Impact:** Low encoding requirement. Maximum resources available for storage and retrieval.7

### **CLS 2: Low-Moderate Resource Demand**

* **Characteristics:** Standard pacing (3-5s per shot). Low Information Introduced (e.g., simple distance changes). Redundant on-screen text.  
* **Cognitive Impact:** ORs effectively guide attention without overwhelming the system.10

### **CLS 3: Moderate Resource Demand**

* **Characteristics:** Moderate pacing (1-2s per shot). Speed ramps up to 1.25x. Non-redundant but relevant on-screen text. Simple camera movements (pans/tilts).  
* **Cognitive Impact:** High engagement, but resources required begin to approach resources allocated. Potential for minor storage degradation if content is complex.24

### **CLS 4: High Resource Demand**

* **Characteristics:** Rapid pacing (\<1s per shot). High Information Introduced (Vii \> 4 per cut). Muted unrelated split-screen elements. High-arousal content (loud noises, intense motion).  
* **Cognitive Impact:** Major load on encoding. High risk of storage failure. Slower STRTs indicate near-capacity processing.9

### **CLS 5: Critical Load / Overload Potential**

* **Characteristics:** Extremely rapid cuts. Speed ramps \>1.5x. Simultaneous competing visual and auditory data (e.g., loud music \+ non-redundant text \+ complex split-screen). Highly arousing, aversive content.  
* **Cognitive Impact:** Cognitive overload. STRTs may paradoxically speed up as encoding fails. Memory recognition (![][image2]) drops significantly.11

## **Interaction Matrix for Processing Load**

To accurately assign a final CLS to a video template, the structural features must be cross-referenced with the content features.

| Structural Load (Template) | Content: Calm/Neutral | Content: Highly Arousing |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Simple (Cuts \>3s)** | Total Load: **Very Low** | Total Load: **Moderate** |
| **Standard (Cuts 1-3s)** | Total Load: **Low-Moderate** | Total Load: **High** |
| **Complex (Cuts \<1s)** | Total Load: **High** | Total Load: **CRITICAL OVERLOAD** |

The data confirms that as arousal increases, the system's tolerance for structural complexity decreases.9 A template that is "High Load" for a calm product explanation becomes "Critical Overload" when applied to a high-intensity action sequence or a fear-based health warning.

## **Synthesis and Recommendations for Media Production**

The application of Lang’s LC4MP to short-form media reveals that cognitive load is not a static property of an editing effect but a dynamic interaction between structural "calls for resources" and content-driven "allocation of resources".2

For short-form video creators, the objective should be the optimization of **processing fluency**—the subjective ease with which a viewer processes a message.7 This is achieved not by removing all complexity, but by strategically managing the load on encoding and storage. Techniques such as maintaining a single recognizable face, using redundant text-audio cues, and slowing the pace during high-arousal moments serve to "protect" the limited cognitive resources of the viewer.7

By adopting the Vii dimensions and STRT-based thresholds, the current "Cognitive Load Score" can be transformed into a predictive tool. This allows for the design of video templates that are "instantly legible" in the high-velocity mobile environment, ensuring that the first 400 milliseconds of exposure lead to a positive cognitive imprint rather than an overload-induced skip.7 Future developments should continue to explore the modality-specific nature of these resource pools, particularly as augmented reality and highly immersive 360-degree video begin to redefine the boundaries of perceptual and cognitive load.5
