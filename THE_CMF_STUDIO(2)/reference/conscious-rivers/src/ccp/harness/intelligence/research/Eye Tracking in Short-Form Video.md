# **The Architecture of Attention: Eye Tracking, Cognitive Continuity, and the Optimization of Short-Form Vertical Video**

The evolution of visual storytelling from traditional cinematic canvases to the handheld, vertically-oriented screens of social media has necessitated a fundamental re-evaluation of how human attention is captured, directed, and maintained. At the intersection of cognitive psychology and audiovisual production lies the Attentional Theory of Cinematic Continuity (ATCC), a framework that explains the biological and psychological mechanisms allowing viewers to perceive a series of fragmented images as a unified narrative. As short-form video platforms like TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts dominate contemporary media consumption, understanding the science of gaze behavior—specifically how it is influenced by cuts, visual composition, and on-screen text—is no longer an aesthetic luxury but a structural requirement for effective communication.

## **The Foundations of Attentional Theory and Continuity Editing**

The conceptual bedrock of modern film editing research is Tim J. Smith’s Attentional Theory of Cinematic Continuity, which addresses the "spectator’s illusion" of seeing a continuous piece of action despite the frequent, jarring transitions known as cuts.1 A typical Hollywood film contains between one thousand and two thousand edits, yet viewers remain largely unaware of these transitions, a phenomenon termed "edit blindness".1 Smith posits that this blindness is not accidental but a result of editors' intuitive adherence to "existence constancy"—the psychological experience that objects persist through space and time even when their presence in the visual field is intermittently disrupted.2

The mechanism of edit blindness is rooted in the way the human visual system processes information. Perception is not a continuous stream but a series of discrete snapshots encoded during fixations (when the eyes are still) and stitched together across periods of "saccadic suppression," the brief blindness occurring during rapid eye movements.1 Smith identifies that continuity editing rules, such as the 180-degree rule and match-on-action, serve to minimize the "capture" of attention by the cut itself. When an edit is performed during a period of natural attention withdrawal—such as a saccade or a blink—the visual disruption is masked.2

### **Attentional Synchrony and the Tyranny of Film**

One of the most significant findings in eye-tracking research is the concept of "attentional synchrony." In unedited or naturalistic viewing, different observers look at different parts of a scene at different times. However, professionally edited cinematic content exerts an "exogenous control" over gaze behavior, forcing a high degree of uniformity among viewers.3 This has been described as the "Tyranny of Film Hypothesis," where the filmmaker uses composition, motion, and editing to dictate exactly where and when the audience looks.3

Attentional synchrony is highest in content with fast pacing and clear focal points, such as Hollywood trailers.3 Research indicates that as visual complexity increases and editing speeds up, viewer attention becomes more concentrated.4 Immediately following a cut, there is a pronounced "center bias" as the gaze converges on the middle of the screen to re-orient to the new shot.4 This synchronization is partly driven by low-level cinematic structures, such as sudden motion or high-contrast elements, which provide a predictable "path" for the eye to follow.4

| Gaze Metric | Unedited/Natural Dynamic Scene | Professionally Edited Film |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Attentional Synchrony | Low (individual exploration) | High (uniformity across viewers) 3 |
| Fixation Duration | Highly variable | Shorter immediately after cuts 4 |
| Saccade Amplitude | Smaller, exploratory | Larger, re-orienting after cuts 4 |
| Gaze Distribution | Dispersed across the frame | Clustered near center/focal points 4 |

The "Tyranny of Film" suggests that the filmmaker is the primary architect of the viewer's cognitive experience. By utilizing specific editing conventions, the filmmaker can ensure that the majority of viewers are looking at the same point at the same time, which is essential for narrative comprehension.3 This exogenous control is so powerful that it persists even during repetitive viewings or when the film is played in reverse, indicating that the structural properties of the video—rather than just the narrative content—are driving the gaze.3

### **The Role of Saccadic Suppression and Edit Blindness**

The human experience of a continuous visual world is largely an illusion constructed from momentary snapshots encoded during fixations.1 Fixations occur when the eyes are relatively static, allowing the retina to process high-resolution information at the fovea, the small region of high acuity.5 Between these fixations, rapid eye movements called saccades occur, during which the visual system is effectively blind.1

Edit blindness occurs when a cut is timed to coincide with these periods of blindness or when the narrative engagement is so high that the viewer suffers from inattentional blindness—the failure to notice an unexpected stimulus because attention is engaged elsewhere.1 Smith’s research found that edits constructed according to continuity rules result in greater edit blindness than those that violate these rules.1 Specifically, a quarter of edits joining two viewpoints of the same scene went undetected, and this increased to a third when the edit coincided with a sudden onset of motion.1

### **Cognitive Foundations of Continuity Rules**

The continuity editing rules, such as the 180-degree rule and the match-on-action, are not merely aesthetic choices but are rooted in the brain's need for spatial and temporal consistency.1 The 180-degree rule ensures that the relative position of objects on the screen and the direction of action remain consistent across cuts, preventing disorientation.1 Recent neuroscientific studies using high-density EEG have explored the neural underpinnings of this rule, suggesting that violations of spatial continuity trigger specific neuronal response patterns.2

Continuity editing ensures "existence constancy"—the belief that objects persist through space and time—by creating conditions where the visual disruption of the cut does not capture attention.2 This is achieved by accommodating the natural assumptions and limitations of the human perceptual system.2 By understanding how film differs from reality and why these differences often go unnoticed, cognitive scientists and filmmakers alike can gain deeper insights into the mechanisms of human perception.8

## **The Geometry of Attention: Mobile vs. Cinema Screens**

The transition from the horizontal (16:9) cinema or TV screen to the vertical (9:16) mobile screen alters the fundamental "viewing strategy" of the observer. Mobile eye-tracking research reveals that while the "center bias" remains a robust phenomenon across all screen types, its intensity and distribution patterns shift on handheld devices.10

### **Variations in Center Bias and Screen Real Estate**

Center bias—the tendency to fixate near the middle of a stimulus—is considered the most established phenomenon in eye-tracking studies.10 On a cinema screen, this bias is often a strategy to maximize the utilization of peripheral vision to monitor the wide field of view.11 Peripheral vision provides a basic overview of the scene and supports search behavior, whereas foveal vision is reserved for processing high-resolution details.5

On a mobile screen, which is typically held close to the face and covers a smaller portion of the total visual field, the center bias is often even more pronounced due to "orbital reserve"—the tendency to keep the eyes centered in their sockets for efficiency—and the vertical orientation of the device.11 Research suggests that in 9:16 video, there is a bias towards gaze locations along the vertical midline, with systematically larger vertical variability in gaze locations compared to horizontal variability.14 This vertical stretching of attention means that the "upper-central third" of the frame becomes the primary focal zone.15

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Cinema/TV | 16:9 (Horizontal) | Horizontal midline dominance; extensive peripheral use | High (strategic/efficient) 11 |
| Mobile/Vertical | 9:16 (Vertical) | Vertical midline dominance; clustered upper-central | Very High (orbital reserve) 14 |
| Real-World | N/A | Highly variable; task-dependent; head-gaze coupling | Lower (goal-oriented) 11 |

### **Handheld Proximity and Environmental Distraction**

Mobile viewing differs from cinema viewing in the degree of "active manipulation" of the viewing device. Cinema viewing is largely passive, whereas mobile viewing involves holding the device, which can introduce physical motion and a different set of depth cues.11 Furthermore, mobile environments are often cluttered with real-world distractions. Studies on "smartphones as distractors" show that the mere presence of a phone can lateralize gaze and divert attention away from a primary task, even when the device is turned off.16 This suggests that the mobile viewer's attention is inherently more fragmented and subject to competition from the immediate physical environment.16

Research consistently indicates that spatial attention exhibits systematic biases rather than uniform distribution across the visual field.16 In a free-viewing paradigm on a screen, participants exhibit greater fixation density toward specific spatial locations, typically those that are visually salient or contextually informative.16 The center bias persists regardless of whether visual features are distributed at the center or the periphery, suggesting that it is a fundamental property of the viewing experience rather than just a response to image composition.10

### **Attentional Synchrony in the Mobile Context**

Attentional synchrony—the degree to which different viewers look at the same place at the same time—is highly influenced by the pacing and complexity of the content.3 In short-form video, where editing is rapid and visual hooks are frequent, attentional synchrony is expected to be high.3 However, the smaller screen size of mobile devices means that viewers can cover the entire screen area more quickly, which could lead to more frequent shifts in attention and potentially lower synchrony if the content is not sufficiently engaging.11

Conversely, the vertical orientation and the close proximity of the screen may enhance the exogenous control of the video. The "Tyranny of Film" may be even more pronounced on mobile screens because the visual field is so concentrated, leaving little room for individual exploration.3 In this context, filmmakers must be even more precise in their use of composition and motion to guide the viewer's eye.15

## **The Face as the Primary Semantic Anchor**

In any video content involving human subjects (A-Roll), the face serves as the paramount focal point. Eye-tracking data consistently shows that the face is the first and most frequent area of interest for viewers, regardless of the shot's scale or complexity.4 Within the face, attention is specifically divided between the eyes and the mouth, a dynamic captured by the Eye-Mouth Index (EMI).17

### **The Eye-Mouth Index (EMI) and Speech Processing**

The distribution of gaze between the eyes and the mouth is modulated by the task and the quality of the signal. When a viewer is merely listening to a talker, the eyes are the primary focus as they convey social cues, gaze direction, and emotional intent.17 The posterior superior temporal sulcus (pSTS) has been identified as a key brain region for processing these signals, with subregions preferring either eyes or mouth movements.18

| Facial Region | Information Conveyed | Gaze Dominance | Neural Correlate |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Eyes | Social cues, gaze location, emotion | High in clear, familiar audio | Posterior pSTS 17 |
| Mouth | Speech content, phonemes, visemes | Increases with noise or unfamiliarity | Anterior pSTS 18 |
| Nose/Face Center | Orientation and alignment | Primary landing spot after a cut | Visual processing areas 4 |

Research indicates that the mouth plays a critical role in visual speech segmentation, particularly when the audio signal is obscured by noise or when the language is unfamiliar.17 In these conditions, gaze shifts significantly toward the mouth to extract visual cues that help identify word boundaries.19 This shift in gaze is associated with better performance on speech segmentation tasks, highlighting the mouth's importance in communicative clarity.19

### **Optimal Shot Scale and Emotional Engagement**

The "medium close-up" (MCU)—showing the subject from mid-chest up—is identified as the ideal shot scale for video content involving characters.4 This scale is optimal because it allows the viewer to process both the eyes and the mouth of the character within a single foveal glance, minimizing the need for large, fatiguing saccades across the screen.4 By reducing the visual effort required to gather social and linguistic information, the MCU promotes viewer immersion and "emotional contagion".4

Extreme close-ups, while powerful, can be disruptive because they force the eye to jump between facial features, potentially breaking the emotional connection or obscuring important contextual information.4 Longer shots, such as medium or extreme long shots, may capture temporary interest but make emotional expressions harder to discern, leading to looser gaze clustering among viewers.4

### **The Presence of a Talking Head in Instructional Content**

In educational or instructional videos, the presence of a "talking head" alongside graphic content can be both a benefit and a distraction. While a visible instructor serves as a social cue that can stimulate deeper processing and increase social presence, it also reduces the time spent looking at the actual learning content.21 Research using eye tracking shows that a talking head significantly reduces the number and duration of fixations on newly appearing content.21 However, this reduction in fixation time does not necessarily lead to a loss in learning outcomes, suggesting that the social benefits may compensate for the visual distraction.21

## **Gaze Competition and Multimodal Complexity**

In short-form social media video, the visual field is a complex landscape where multiple elements—talking heads, B-roll imagery, kinetic typography, and platform-specific UI—compete for attention. This competition can be modeled through the lens of cognitive load theory and the specific "attraction" of different visual stimuli.

### **The Competition Between Text and Visual Imagery**

Subtitles and on-screen text are powerful exogenous stimuli that often take priority over visual imagery.22 Research indicates that viewers automatically attend to subtitles on the screen without their volition, a phenomenon driven by both the top-down need for information and the bottom-up salience of newly appearing text.22 A surprisingly large proportion of time—over 40%—can be spent reading captions even when a redundant audio stream is present.23

When subtitles appear, the gaze typically shifts from the center of the screen to the beginning of the text within a fraction of a second.22 This results in a shorter "dwell time" on visual imagery and a longer scan path as viewers must constantly toggle between the main visual area and the text.22 The interference caused by subtitles is so strong that the editing style (continuity vs. discontinuity) becomes a secondary factor in determining gaze behavior.22

| Gaze Metric | Video Without Subtitles | Video With Subtitles |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Mean Fixation Duration | Longer (scene perception) | Shorter (reading process) 22 |
| Scan Path Length | Shorter (focal interest) | Longer (toggling between text/image) 22 |
| Attention on Imagery | High | Reduced (competing with text) 22 |
| Delay to New AOI | Minimal | Significant if subtitle starts before cut 22 |

### **Kinetic Typography: Motion as a Signal**

Kinetic typography—animated text that changes in scale, position, or rotation—is a staple of contemporary short-form video design.24 From a cognitive perspective, motion is one of the most powerful attentional cues, detected rapidly by the visual system within hundreds of milliseconds.24 Kinetic typography leverages this biological hardwiring to capture attention in "mute-first" social media feeds where static text might be ignored.24

However, the effectiveness of kinetic typography depends on its alignment with the narrative and audio pacing. When motion mirrors the emphasis of the speech, it can improve information grouping and segmentation.24 Conversely, when motion is purely decorative or distracting—such as rotating every word without purpose—it increases cognitive load and can hurt recall.24 Minimalist animations that prioritize legibility and subtlety are often more effective for information retention than bold, chaotic designs.25

### **"Safe Zones" and Subtitle Placement in Vertical Video**

For 9:16 vertical video, the placement of subtitles is constrained by the "Safe Zones" defined by platform interfaces (like/comment buttons, descriptions) and natural gaze patterns.

1. **Platform UI Avoidance:** TikTok and Instagram Reels have UI elements that obscure the bottom and right portions of the screen. Placing text in these areas renders it unreadable.15  
2. **The Upper-Central Strategy:** Positioning captions in the "upper-central third" (between 15% and 60% from the top) is increasingly recommended.15 This places the text closer to the speaker's face, reducing the distance the eyes must travel and keeping the text in the viewer's natural focal zone when holding a phone vertically.15  
3. **The Bottom-Third Rule vs. Reality:** While the industry standard has often been the "bottom-third," this must be modified for mobile to avoid UI overlap. Captions should be kept within the middle 60% to 80% of the screen width to ensure they are not cut off by varying device aspect ratios.28

| Feature | Vertical Positioning | Practical Advice |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Primary Face/Eyes | 15% \- 25% from top | Keep subject eyes in this zone 15 |
| Main Captions | 30% \- 60% from top | Optimal for readability and UI avoidance 15 |
| System UI Buttons | Bottom 20% & Far Right | Avoid placing any text here 28 |
| Notification Clearances | Top 10% | Avoid for critical information 29 |

## **The Mechanics of the "Invisible Cut"**

In the context of programmatic video editing, the "timing" of effects and transitions can be optimized to align with the viewer's natural attentional patterns. Research into "safe cuts" suggests that edits are least disruptive when they occur during periods of sensory suppression or when they are masked by other visual changes.

### **Saccades, Blinks, and Saccadic Suppression**

As established, the human eye is "blind" during a saccade, a period known as saccadic suppression.1 This period, along with the duration of an eyeblink, provides an ideal window for a cut.1 Smith’s research suggests that while some cuts are missed due to suppression during these biological events, the majority of edit blindness is due to inattentional blindness as viewers focus on the narrative.1

However, vertical eye orientation can systematically confound the measurement of these events. For instance, downward eye orientation—common when holding a mobile device—can increase measured blink duration by up to 32% compared to upward orientation.12 This variability in "blind windows" means that editors must be careful when timing cuts to biological markers, as the physical position of the viewer's head and eyes can change the effective duration of these masks.12

### **Match-on-Action and Attentional Focus**

A "Match-Cut" (TR-07) or "match-on-action" is most successful when it maintains the position of the "attentional focus" across the cut.1 If the viewer is looking at a character's hand in one shot, and the next shot places the hand in the same screen position, the viewer does not need to perform a "search saccade" to find the subject in the new frame.1 This preservation of spatial continuity is essential for maintaining immersion and the illusion of a continuous event.2

Programmatic systems can utilize this principle by aligning key features (like eyes or products) across transitions. By ensuring that the "landing point" of the gaze after a cut is the same as the "starting point" before the cut, the disruption is minimized.4

### **Neural Responses to Novelty and Semantic Boundaries**

Intracranial recordings from humans show that neural responses to film cuts are widespread across the brain and are modulated by "semantic novelty".30 Cuts that occur at semantic event boundaries (e.g., when a character enters a new room) are particularly effective at triggering responses in the temporal and medial temporal lobes, which are involved in memory and narrative comprehension.30 This suggests that cuts should not just be timed to biological blinks but should align with the "semantic structure" of the content to be most effective.30

## **Procedural Attention Direction through Effects**

Specific visual effects and motion dynamics can be used to "steer" the viewer's gaze toward specific information. This is particularly useful in short-form video where the viewer's attention must be "hooked" within the first 1.5 to 3 seconds to prevent them from scrolling away.15

### **Spotlight Effects and Luminance Salience**

A "Spotlight effect" (C-07) functions as a powerful exogenous cue by darkening the periphery and increasing the relative luminance of a focal area. This exploits the visual system's preference for high-contrast and high-luminance stimuli, effectively "forcing" the eye toward the highlighted region.4 In a complex vertical composition, this can be used to direct the gaze away from a talking head toward a specific text overlay or product feature.34

### **Punch-In Zooms and Focal Magnification**

"Punch-In" zooms (M-04) are used to isolate visually "punctual" elements.34 By suddenly enlarging a specific area—such as a character's eyes during an emotional beat—the editor creates a powerful "onset of motion" that triggers a saccadic re-orientation toward that point.1 This technique is also used to save continuity issues in low-budget productions by allowing for a "re-crop" that maintains focal consistency across different takes.35

### **Speed Ramps and Gaze Concentration**

Speed ramping—varying the frame rate within a single shot—acts as a temporal cue that influences gaze stability. Research indicates that drivers, for example, have more concentrated gaze distributions and longer gaze durations when they can effectively gather information from their environment.36 In video editing, decelerating into slow motion "concentrates" attention on a specific action, allowing the viewer to encode details that would be lost at normal speeds.36 Conversely, accelerating motion can "scatter" attention, signaling that the current sequence is less important and preparing the viewer for an upcoming transition.36

### **Camera Shake and Motivating Motion**

Camera shake or "handheld" effects are often used to simulate energy, intensity, or a sense of presence.31 However, from an attentional perspective, excessive camera shake can scatter gaze and lead to "motion sickness".38 To maintain gaze stability, a clear "center of interest" must be maintained even as the frame moves around it. Gimbals are often used to give a sense of momentum without the distracting and fatiguing effects of raw camera shake.38

## **Subtitle Design and Readability Metrics**

For short-form video, subtitles are not just an accessibility feature; they are a primary delivery mechanism for information, especially since over 80% of social media video is watched on mute.26 The design of these captions—font choice, size, color, and pacing—must be optimized for the constraints of mobile viewing.

### **Typography and Contrast Requirements**

Sans-serif fonts are standard for digital readability due to their superior legibility and faster comprehension at small sizes.29 Contrast is the single most important factor in readability, with white text on a black background providing a 21:1 contrast ratio that exceeds all accessibility guidelines.29

## **Individual Differences in Visual Attention**

It is important to recognize that not all viewers process dynamic scenes in the same way. Individual differences in visual attention skills—such as those trained through video gaming—can affect how people interact with fast-paced, cluttered content.41

1. **Video Gamers:** Active gamers often have improved target detection and can attend to visual targets across a wider area of the scene.41 They are better at shifting from diffused to focused attention states, which may make them more resilient to "discontinuity" editing.41  
2. **Cognitive Ability:** Higher cognitive ability, as indicated by measures like general intelligence and working memory capacity, is associated with a tendency to focus attention more centrally within a scene.41  
3. **Language and Proficiency:** Viewers process subtitles in different languages differently, with native speakers often skipping subtitles more frequently than those watching in a foreign language.22 This affects the total "dwell time" on visual imagery versus text.22

## **Programmatic Implications and Strategic Recommendations**

The synthesis of eye-tracking data and cognitive film theory provides a clear set of principles for optimizing short-form vertical video. By aligning production choices with the biological and psychological realities of human attention, creators can maximize engagement and comprehension.

* **Focal Alignment across Cuts:** Transitions should be designed to keep the viewer’s gaze on the same region of the screen before and after the cut.1 Programmatic tools should align key features—such as eyes or product logos—across shot changes to maintain spatial continuity and existence constancy.2  
* **Vertical Hierarchy of Attention:** In 9:16 video, the primary visual interest (the face) should be placed in the "upper-central third" (15-40% from the top).15 Subtitles and captions should be positioned in the zone immediately below the speaker's eyes (30-60% from the top) to minimize the vertical distance the eyes must travel and to avoid platform UI elements.15  
* **Timing to Suppression Windows:** Edits should be timed to coincide with natural attentional lulls, such as those triggered by blinks or the onset of major visual changes.1 While real-time blink detection is a burgeoning field for mobile devices, using "motion transients" (sudden movements or flashes) can exogenously trigger a saccade, providing a natural mask for a cut.1  
* **Motion-Driven Text Highlights:** Kinetic typography should be used to emphasize key phrases, leveraging the brain's hardwired sensitivity to motion.24 To avoid cognitive overload, motion motifs should be consistent and mirror the pacing or semantic emphasis of the audio.24  
* **Contrast-Driven Spotlight:** In scenes with multiple competing elements, use luminance contrast and spotlight effects to guide the gaze toward the most important information.4 This prevents the viewer from being overwhelmed by visual complexity and ensures clear narrative progression.  
* **Shot Scale for Immersive Emotion:** Prioritize "medium close-ups" for talking-head content to allow simultaneous foveal processing of the eyes and mouth, facilitating emotional contagion and reducing cognitive effort.4  
* **Platform-Specific Safe Zones:** Always design within the "Safe Zones" of the target platform, ensuring that no critical text or action is obscured by like/comment buttons or system notifications.15

By grounding short-form video production in the science of attention, creators can move beyond purely aesthetic choices toward a more sophisticated, "neurologically-aligned" form of storytelling. This approach respects the inherent limitations of the human visual system while leveraging its most powerful survival mechanisms—the detection of motion, the processing of faces, and the drive for spatial and temporal continuity.
